    The loop

    One protocol, once a day, any domain. Nothing else for the first twenty-one days.

    1. Name it

      One sentence, physical and observable. "Wash three dishes," never "clean the house." "Read pages 4–7," never "study."

    2. Run the launch ritual

      Same spot, same starting track, phone to the other room, touch the task physically — file open, object in hand. Sixty seconds, identical every day. Repetition moves starting from motivation to environment: the ritual is the cue, not a warm-up. If it fails twice in a row on the same task, a person is the escalation — fallback, never the default.

    3. Timer on, phone in another room

      When a thought pulls you away, park it on the Later list and return for two more minutes. Returning counts as success, not failure.

    4. Stop on time

      Even mid-momentum. A session that ends on schedule teaches you sessions are cheap — which is exactly the belief perfectionism attacks.

    5. Mark the day

      Started or didn't. No journaling, no quality score, nothing else.

    Why this works: a tiny first action lowers the cost of starting below the cost of avoiding. You are not training productivity — you are training the transition from intention to action. Researching methods, apps, or note systems is allowed only after the day's session.

    The thoughts that block step 1

    Before tapping, try to produce the counter yourself — that's retrieval practice on your own self-talk. Then say the script out loud and execute the move within thirty seconds. The script buys the window; the action is the intervention.

    "Preparation is replacing action. Ten ordinary minutes first."

    Start the timer with the ordinary method. Research is allowed after the session, never before.

    "I don't need to want it. I need five minutes and a smaller target."

    Shrink the target to one visible result — three dishes, one clear chair. Add music or a call.

    "The task is not the unit. The start is the unit."

    Write the smallest physical first action and do only that. The rest is not today's problem.

    "Returning is the skill I'm training. A good session can include several returns."

    Park the pulling thought on the Later list. Return for two minutes. That counts.

    "A bridge beats silence."

    Send now: "I saw this. I'm busy, but I'll reply properly soon." Schedule the real reply if it matters.

    "I don't wait for pressure. I manufacture it."

    Text one person: "Starting 10 minutes of X now, will confirm when done." Then start.

    "The demo date does the teaching. I schedule the display, then prepare it."

    Put a five-minute explanation on the calendar — a teammate, a doc, a post — then start today's session as prep for it. Being visibly mid-learning is the non-douchy version of the same fuel.

    The fuel

    The honest motive: knowing more than the people around me, and showing it. Unmanaged, it rewards appearing knowledgeable and punishes being a visible beginner — perfectionist delay in a new outfit. Harnessed, it becomes the protégé effect: schedule the display before doing the work.

    The mechanic: commit today to a five-minute explanation on a named date — a teammate, an internal doc, a post. Every session between now and then is preparing the performance, not "studying." The payoff moves from someday-mastery to a deadline with an audience you chose.

    G1
    Explains well, not knows more

    The sustainable frame is being the person who explains things well. Same fuel, generosity-framed — it compounds instead of costing relationships.

    G2
    Measure by can-build

    Never by can-talk-about. The motive will happily let those two diverge, and only one of them is real.

    G3
    One domain of visible beginnerhood

    Kept permanently, as vaccination — without it the motive quietly steers you away from everything where you would look novice.

    Two missed days in a row

    Not a character verdict — data that the action is still too big. Downshift in this order:

    01
    Halve the timer

    Ten minutes becomes five. Five becomes two. There is no floor too low.

    02
    Change location

    Different room, café, desk. Context carries habit; break the context that carries avoidance.

    03
    Do it live on a call

    If solo starting keeps failing, stop starting solo. Full body doubling, every session.

    Never respond to a missed day by redesigning the system. One variable, in order, nothing else.

    The record

    Started days, last 21. After day one, the streak is what each new day protects.

    Clear the Later list weekly: schedule, do, or delete every item.

    Day 21: the decision

    Three weeks, not two — the ritual lever needs the longer runway to condition. An experiment still needs a branch at the end. Count your started days:

    10 / 21
    8–14 · Signal, not yet habit Keep the ritual identical and run 21 more days. If starting is not getting easier by then, switch the lever.

    The thresholds are judgment calls, not research-derived cutoffs. If your baseline was near zero, even four started days may justify the "it works" branch. Adjust to your own baseline — but decide something at day 21, and judge two things: the count, and whether starting is getting easier. An experiment without a decision is a diary.

    If the count lands under 8 of 21, that result is useful: friction-reduction alone isn't enough, and a professional evaluation (executive function, ADHD, anxiety and perfectionism, mood, sleep) is the next step — not more self-help. Educational, not medical advice. Seek prompt help for severe depression, hopelessness, or thoughts of self-harm.
